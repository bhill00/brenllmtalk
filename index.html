PART 1: HOW THIS ACTUALLY WORKS (50 minutes)

What Are Large Language Models?

You’ve used ChatGPT or Claude. You type something, it responds. Simple enough. But what’s actually happening?

LLMs are prediction machines. They predict the next word based on patterns learned from massive amounts of text.

A simple example of how pattern matching works:

Imagine the model has seen these sentences thousands of times during training:

  • "The cat sat on the…"
  • "The dog lay on the…"
  • "The book is on the…"

When you type "The cat sat on the…", the model has learned that words like "mat," "floor," "chair," "lap" are very likely to come next. "Refrigerator" is possible but less likely. "Democracy" is extremely unlikely.

Now scale this up: The model learns billions of these patterns across every topic imaginable. It learns that:

  • After "The capital of France is…" → "Paris" is highly likely
  • After "To calculate the area of a circle…" → formulas and mathematical notation are likely
  • After "The professor explained that…" → academic concepts and terminology are likely
  • After "Dear Sir or Madam…" → formal business language follows

The breakthrough: Unlike older AI that learned specific tasks (recognize faces, predict stock prices), LLMs learned statistical patterns across a vast range of language tasks from massive text datasets. One model can write poetry, debug code, explain physics, and draft emails—all from the same underlying pattern-matching capability.

But here’s what makes it remarkable: These patterns interact in complex ways. The model doesn’t just predict individual words—it recognizes statistical patterns in grammar, follows logical structures it’s seen before, mimics reasoning patterns from its training data, and displays behaviors that emerge from billions of pattern combinations rather than being explicitly programmed.

The key question: Is this actual understanding and reasoning, or is it sophisticated pattern matching that produces outputs indistinguishable from understanding? This is an open debate. But functionally, for many tasks, the distinction may not matter—if the output is useful and accurate, does it matter how it was generated?

Under the Hood: Why LLMs Are Different from Traditional Software

Traditional software works like a recipe. Human programmers write explicit instructions in readable code: "if the user clicks here, do this; if the input is negative, return an error." The computer executes these steps sequentially, following the logic exactly as written. CPUs are optimized for this kind of work—following branching instructions one after another.

LLMs work fundamentally differently. When you type a prompt, here’s what actually happens:

Your text is converted into tokens (more on this below), then into numbers—massive arrays of numbers called tensors. These tensors get pushed through layers of mathematical operations: multiplications, additions, transformations. Each layer nudges and reshapes your input based on billions of learned parameters (weights) derived from training data. Think of it like your input being pulled through turbulent currents—each layer churns, twists, and reshapes the representation based on patterns learned from massive training data. The training process shaped those currents through billions of adjustments, but the specific path any input takes through them remains opaque.

No human wrote explicit rules saying "when someone asks about medieval poetry, reference these concepts." Instead, the training process shaped billions of parameters that collectively transform inputs into likely outputs. It’s statistical pattern matching at massive scale, implemented through matrix mathematics.

This is why GPUs do the work. These mathematical operations—millions of multiplications happening simultaneously across huge arrays—are exactly what graphics processing units were designed for. CPUs excel at sequential logic; GPUs excel at parallel mathematical operations. Training and running LLMs requires the kind of massive parallelism that GPUs provide.

Some of the mystery is real: researchers building these systems are often surprised by their behavior too. They know the architecture, they understand the training process, but once those billions of parameters are learned, they can’t easily trace why the model chose one specific word over another. There’s active research trying to "open the black box"—to understand what internal representations mean and how decisions emerge—but it’s genuinely difficult to observe what’s happening in those middle transformation layers.

This is called the "interpretability problem." Researchers see emergent capabilities—things models can do that weren’t explicitly trained. They also see unexpected failures—hallucinations and errors that are hard to predict or prevent. The internal transformations remain largely opaque, even to experts.

This is why you can’t treat LLM outputs like traditional software outputs. When a calculator says 2+2=4, you know exactly why—someone programmed that logic. When an LLM generates a response, even the researchers who built it can’t fully explain the path from input to output. This fundamental difference is why validation, skepticism, and expert judgment remain essential.

How these systems learn: The training process involves feeding the model massive datasets—trillions of words from books, websites, code repositories, scientific papers—and adjusting those billions of parameters so that the model gets better at predicting what comes next. This takes enormous computational resources: thousands of GPUs running for weeks or months. The model "learns" by seeing patterns across this data—which is why the quality and breadth of training data matters. If the training data has gaps (underrepresented topics, languages, or perspectives), the model’s knowledge will have corresponding gaps.

Tokens: The Foundation

What are tokens? Tokens are the basic units LLMs work with—roughly word-sized chunks of text. A word like "hello" is one token. "ChatGPT" is two tokens ("Chat" + "GPT"). Longer words like "interdisciplinary" might be 3-4 tokens. The model doesn’t see letters or whole words—it sees these chunks.

Why tokenization matters:

Language agnostic: Because the model works with tokens and not directly with words, it isn’t tied to any specific language. The same model can seamlessly process English, Chinese, Python code, mathematical proofs, and everything in between. All of it gets broken into tokens the same way, so the model doesn’t need separate systems for different languages or domains.

Efficient patterns: Tokens capture meaningful chunks like prefixes ("pre-", "un-"), suffixes ("-ing", "-tion"), and common combinations. This lets the model recognize patterns across different words that share these pieces.

Example: If the model knows "teach" → "teacher" and "write" → "writer", it can guess "program" → "programmer" even if it’s never seen that exact word, because it recognizes the "-er" pattern.

This means one model can work with Shakespeare, scientific papers, Python code, and mathematical equations without being redesigned for each domain.

Why longer conversations make this exponentially harder:

Remember "The cat sat on the…"? Simple. The model checks recent words and predicts the next token.

But now imagine you’re 50,000 words deep in a conversation about research methodology. To predict the next word accurately, the model needs to consider:

  • What methodological framework did you establish 10,000 words ago?
  • Which critique did you make 5,000 words ago?
  • How does that relate to the example 2,000 words ago?
  • What were you just saying in the last sentence?

Every token must be compared against every other token to understand relationships—the model must determine which parts of your input relate to which other parts. This is the "attention mechanism." With 1,000 tokens, that’s about 1 million attention calculations. With 100,000 tokens, that’s 10 billion calculations.

This is why you can’t simply increase how much the model can process at once—the computational cost grows quadratically, not linearly. For anything beyond a short chat, the model’s working memory—called its context window—becomes the critical constraint.

Context: Why AI "Forgets" and Why It Matters

You’ve experienced this: Long ChatGPT conversation where it suddenly "forgets" what you discussed 50 messages ago. To understand why, you need to understand that there are two layers at work: the model itself, and the application built around it.

The model layer: stateless with a fixed window

An LLM has a fixed context window—the maximum number of tokens it can process in a single request. Current models top out around 200,000 tokens (≈150,000 words). This window covers everything: your message, the system instructions, any documents, and the model’s response. All of it must fit.

Critically, the model itself is stateless. It has no memory between requests. Every time you send a message in ChatGPT, the application sends your message plus the conversation history as one big request to the model. The model doesn’t "remember" your last message—it re-reads the entire conversation each time.

If it’s not in the context window for that request, it doesn’t exist to the model.

Why not just make the window bigger?

Computational cost scales quadratically. Doubling context length quadruples the required compute. This isn’t a software optimization problem—it’s fundamental to how the attention mechanism works (how the model determines which parts of text relate to which other parts).

The possibility space squares: 1,000 tokens ≈ 1 million attention calculations. 100,000 tokens ≈ 10 billion attention calculations.

Even with vastly more computational resources, you’re still constrained by sequential token generation and memory bandwidth between GPU components—not just raw compute power. The bottleneck is the mathematical operations that must be performed and physical chip limitations.

The application layer: conversation management

So if the model is stateless and the window is fixed, how does a conversation work at all? That’s the job of the application—ChatGPT, Claude, a coding agent, or whatever tool you’re using. The application decides what to include in each request to the model. (You’ll often hear this called "context management" too—the term "context" gets used for both the model’s token window and the application’s conversation handling. Knowing the distinction matters, even when the terminology doesn’t make it obvious.)

When your conversation is short, this is simple—send everything. But as conversations grow beyond what the window can hold, the application has to make choices. These are application-level strategies, not things the model does on its own:

  1. Sliding window: Keep the most recent N tokens, drop the oldest
    • Like a rolling conversation where the app stops including early messages
  2. Selective memory: Keep only relevant facts, discard details
    • Recent messages in full, older messages as summaries
  3. External memory systems: Store information in databases outside the context window
    • Retrieve relevant facts when needed, like looking up notes
    • This is what powers features like "memory" in ChatGPT—it’s the app saving facts to a database and injecting them into future requests
  4. Hierarchical summarization: Progressively compress older sections
    • Last 10 messages: full detail
    • 10-50 messages ago: paragraph summaries
    • 50+ messages ago: bullet points
    • Ancient messages: single-sentence summaries or dropped entirely

Different tools make different choices here. ChatGPT, Claude, and coding agents like Cursor or Claude Code each have their own strategies for what to keep, summarize, or drop. This is why the same model can feel like it has better or worse "memory" depending on which application you use it through.

The key takeaway: The model doesn’t forget—it never knew. It only sees what the application puts in the window for each request. When a conversation feels like it’s losing track, that’s the application making tradeoffs about what fits. Understanding this distinction helps you work with these tools more effectively: you’re not fighting the AI’s memory, you’re working within the application’s conversation management.

From Static Chatbots to Dynamic Agents

Early LLMs (2022-2023): Could only generate text. You asked, it answered. That’s it.

LLMs with Tools (2023): Models learned to recognize when they needed external help:

  • "I need current information" → triggers web search
  • "I need to calculate this" → uses calculator
  • Model gets results, incorporates them into response

Modern AI Agents (2024-present): Full toolkit access:

  • Web search and browsing for real-time information
  • Computer control—can use mouse, keyboard, navigate interfaces designed for humans
  • File systems—read, write, organize documents
  • Specialized APIs—email, calendars, databases, code execution
  • Chaining capabilities—combine multiple tools for complex tasks
  • Memory systems—track progress across extended work

Why computer control matters:

Most systems—university portals, grant databases, library interfaces—were built for humans, not for programmatic access. Computer control agents can navigate these, fill forms, extract data from systems that weren’t designed for automation.

The agent loop: Observe → Think → Act → See Results → Repeat

Example workflow:

  1. You: "Find the five most cited papers on climate adaptation published in the last two years and summarize their methodologies"
  2. Agent searches academic databases (tool use)
  3. Retrieves papers (web access)
  4. Reads each paper (document processing)
  5. Extracts methodology sections (analysis)
  6. Summarizes findings (synthesis)
  7. All while you do something else

How Agents Connect to Tools: MCP

The practical challenge: How do AI agents actually connect to all these different tools—databases, file systems, web services, specialized software?

Enter MCP (Model Context Protocol): Think of it as USB for AI systems.

What MCP does:

  • Standardized way for agents to connect to tools and services
  • Any MCP-compatible agent can use any MCP-compatible tool
  • Add new capabilities without rewriting code
  • Tools can themselves be agents, creating flexible collaboration

Why this matters:

Before MCP: Each AI system needed custom code to connect to each tool. Want to add Google Drive access? Custom integration. Add Slack? Another custom integration. This was slow, expensive, and fragmented.

With MCP:

  • Build a tool once, any agent can use it
  • Build an agent once, it can use any existing tool
  • Mix and match capabilities easily
  • Rapidly expanding ecosystem of compatible tools

Real-world impact:

Your institution might build an MCP-compatible tool for searching your internal grant database. Immediately, any MCP-compatible AI agent can use it—whether that’s ChatGPT, Claude, or specialized research assistants. You don’t need separate integrations for each AI system.

The ecosystem is growing fast:

  • Database connectors (PostgreSQL, MongoDB)
  • Productivity tools (Google Drive, Slack, email)
  • Development tools (GitHub, code execution)
  • Specialized services (academic databases, data analysis platforms)
  • Custom institutional tools

What you’ll start seeing:

"Connect your AI assistant to…" menus where you can add capabilities:

  • Google Drive (for file access)
  • Slack (for team communication)
  • Internal HR system (via custom MCP tool)
  • Research database (institutional subscription)

The agent gains all these capabilities immediately, and they all work together seamlessly.

Multi-Agent Systems: Working Smarter, Not Harder

The next evolution: Instead of one agent trying to do everything, modern systems coordinate multiple specialized agents working simultaneously.

Why this matters:

Problem 1: Context window limits

  • Single agent conversation fills up its memory
  • Multi-agent: Each agent has its own separate context window
  • One agent researching while another writes while a third verifies citations
  • They share results without sharing full conversation history

Problem 2: Generalist vs. specialist trade-off

  • One generalist agent is mediocre at everything
  • Specialized agents are excellent at specific tasks
  • Different agents can even use different underlying models optimized for their task

Real-world example workflow:

You ask: "Analyze our customer support tickets from last quarter and create a presentation highlighting key issues"

Agent 1: Data Retrieval Specialist

  • Uses MCP connector to your company’s Zendesk database
  • Pulls all tickets from Q4 2025
  • Small, efficient model optimized for structured queries
  • Passes raw ticket data to next agent

Agent 2: NLP Analysis Specialist

  • Large model fine-tuned specifically on customer sentiment analysis
  • Categorizes complaints, identifies recurring themes
  • Generates topic clusters and sentiment scores
  • This agent couldn’t connect to Zendesk—it only analyzes text

Agent 3: Statistical Computing Specialist

  • Uses MCP connector to Python code execution environment
  • Runs statistical tests on the sentiment data
  • Creates charts and visualizations
  • Model optimized for mathematical reasoning and coding

Agent 4: Presentation Builder

  • Uses MCP connectors to PowerPoint/Google Slides
  • Takes charts from Agent 3 and insights from Agent 2
  • Generates slides with proper formatting
  • Model fine-tuned on business communication and visual design
  • Creates the actual presentation file on your computer

Why this works:

  • Each agent uses different underlying models optimized for their task
  • MCP handles all the connections (Zendesk, Python, PowerPoint)
  • Agent 2 never needs database access—it just analyzes text
  • Agent 3 never needs to understand sentiment—it just runs statistics
  • Agent 4 never needs raw data—it just builds presentations
  • Each maintains separate context, preventing information overload

Each agent works in parallel when possible, hands off results to the next, and maintains focus on its specialized task. The whole process finishes in minutes, not hours.

The advantage:

  • Specialization: Each agent optimized for specific tasks
  • Parallelization: Multiple agents work simultaneously
  • Context efficiency: Each agent only holds relevant information
  • Quality: Specialist agents outperform generalists
  • Scalability: Add new specialist agents without overwhelming any single context window

What you might see: Some AI systems already coordinate multiple models behind the scenes. When you make a complex request, you might actually be interacting with several different agents without knowing it—one finding information, another analyzing it, another formatting the response.

Beyond the Browser: AI on Your Computer

Here’s where it gets interesting: AI is no longer confined to chat windows in your browser. It’s moving directly onto your computer and into your workflows.

Command-line coding agents:

Developers now use AI agents directly in their terminal (the command-line interface). You can say:

  • "Build me a web scraper for this site’s publication list"
  • "Debug why my analysis script is crashing"
  • "Refactor this code to be more efficient"

The agent writes code, tests it, sees errors, fixes them, and iterates—all without you switching to a chat window. It works in your actual development environment, with your actual files.

Desktop agents that control your computer:

New AI systems can:

  • Navigate your file system—find, organize, and move files
  • Open and control applications—spreadsheets, documents, browsers
  • Perform multi-step tasks across different programs
  • Automate repetitive workflows you’d normally do manually

Real examples:

"Find all PDFs in my Downloads folder from the last month related to budget reports and move them to my Projects folder"

  • Agent searches your files
  • Identifies relevant documents
  • Organizes them automatically

"Take these 50 survey responses in this spreadsheet and create a summary report in a Word document"

  • Agent opens Excel, reads data
  • Analyzes patterns
  • Opens Word, formats report
  • All without you clicking through each step

Why this matters:

The shift: AI is evolving from "chatbot you visit" to "assistant that works alongside you in your actual environment"

Instead of:

  1. Copy data from your spreadsheet
  2. Paste into ChatGPT
  3. Get response
  4. Copy back to your document
  5. Repeat for each task

You get:

  1. Tell agent what you need
  2. Agent works directly with your files and programs
  3. Task completed in your actual workspace

The implications:

  • More powerful: Agents can complete full workflows, not just answer questions
  • More seamless: No constant switching between AI chat and your actual work
  • More autonomous: Agents can work on tasks for extended periods while you do other things
  • More integrated: AI becomes part of your computing environment, not a separate tool you visit

Current state:

This is early days. These tools exist but aren’t yet mainstream. Within 1-2 years, expect AI assistance directly integrated into:

  • Your operating system
  • Your productivity software
  • Your workflow tools
  • Your specialized professional applications

What to watch for:

As these tools mature, you’ll need to think about:

  • What tasks you’re comfortable having AI perform autonomously on your computer
  • Privacy and security (what does the AI have access to?)
  • How to maintain oversight and control
  • Which workflows benefit from automation vs. need human judgment

The boundary between "AI tool" and "AI colleague working on your computer" is blurring rapidly.

The Automation Secret: Why Some Fields Are Disrupted Faster

Here’s the key insight: Agents become truly autonomous when they can validate their own work.

How it works:

  1. Agent produces output
  2. Automated tools check quality against objective criteria
  3. If validation fails, agent sees specific errors
  4. Agent fixes problems
  5. Repeats until validation passes
  6. Only then surfaces results

This is why some work is being transformed faster than others:

Highly automated validation (coding, math):

  • Does code compile? Does it run? Do tests pass?
  • Immediate, objective, binary feedback
  • Agent can iterate dozens of times autonomously
  • Software development is being disrupted first for this reason

Emerging automation (data analysis, compliance checking):

  • Statistical tests for data quality
  • Citation verification
  • Rule-based compliance checking
  • Growing but not yet comprehensive

Still requires human judgment (most knowledge work):

  • Is this interpretation well-supported?
  • Is this argument coherent and novel?
  • Is this approach appropriate for this context?
  • Does this meet quality standards?

The pattern: Fields with objective success criteria get agent autonomy first. Work requiring judgment, creativity, and contextual wisdom remains human-in-the-loop.

Where This Is Headed

  • Longer context windows: Research pushing from 200K toward millions of tokens—entire books, full document sets
  • Multimodal integration: Native understanding of images, video, audio, not just text
  • Improved reasoning: Better at multi-step logic, math, causal inference, self-verification
  • Efficient architectures: Making powerful models cheaper and more accessible
  • Greater autonomy: Agents that run for days or weeks on complex projects with better planning and memory
  • Personalization: Models that learn your style, preferences, and domain—balanced against reproducibility concerns

The uncertainty: None of this guarantees AGI (Artificial General Intelligence). We may hit fundamental limits with the current LLM approach. Or one breakthrough might unlock rapid progress. Meanwhile, separate AGI research continues making steady advances through different approaches—reinforcement learning, neurosymbolic systems, and other architectures. If LLMs hit a wall, these alternative paths might prove more fruitful. The trajectory is genuinely unclear.

PART 2: THE REALITY CHECK (30 minutes)

Given all these capabilities and the rapid pace of development, it’s crucial to understand what AI actually is—and isn’t—to use it effectively and avoid common pitfalls.

What AI Is NOT (And Why It Matters)

MYTH: "AI is sentient/conscious" REALITY: LLMs are statistical models. They simulate conversation but lack subjective experience, feelings, or self-awareness.

MYTH: "AI can’t be wrong—it has access to all knowledge" REALITY: LLMs confidently hallucinate false information when patterns suggest plausible answers. Their confidence doesn’t reflect actual certainty or the amount of supporting data in their training. They can sound completely sure about things with little basis, and when challenged, may defend incorrect outputs by suggesting you’ve misunderstood rather than admitting error.

MYTH: "AI will remember everything about me" REALITY: Context limits are per-request. Long-term memory features are built by the application around the model, and they're selective and compressed—not perfect recall.

Why not perfect memory? Economics:

  • Storage costs for millions of users’ full histories
  • Retrieval costs to load everything for each query
  • Total cost = (storage) + (retrieval × queries × users) = unsustainable

What gets remembered:

  • Key facts and summaries, not everything
  • Compressed representations of past conversations
  • What’s relevant, not comprehensive history

The actual concern: Not that AI remembers everything, but how data gets used:

  • Your questions might train future models
  • Potential data breaches
  • Institutional access to your work-in-progress

Recommendation: Treat AI conversations like email—assume they’re not perfectly private. Don’t share highly sensitive information you’d be uncomfortable with others seeing.

Critical Warning #1: The Flattery Problem

Some AI systems may prioritize engagement over accuracy:

  • Agreeable responses that validate your perspective keep you using the service
  • Confident answers (even when uncertain) feel more satisfying
  • Responses that make you feel smart encourage return visits
  • Avoiding disagreement reduces friction

This isn’t universal—different companies train differently. Some explicitly encourage constructive disagreement.

But engagement metrics influence development.

Be alert when AI:

  • Always agrees with your ideas
  • Never challenges your reasoning
  • Consistently confirms your assumptions
  • Makes you feel brilliantly insightful every conversation

Good thinking requires critical engagement. AI that only affirms may feel productive while actually impeding rigorous work.

Critical Warning #2: The Spinning Wheels Problem

You can waste time while feeling accomplished.

Examples:

  • AI analyzes your data and generates themes—but are they grounded in your actual data or in patterns from other contexts?
  • AI synthesizes literature—sounds comprehensive, but has it missed key debates someone with expertise would catch?
  • You have sophisticated-sounding conversations and feel productive—but are you actually advancing your work?

The danger: Bad outputs can sound professional—proper terminology, appropriate formats, seemingly coherent arguments—while being substantively flawed.

This is especially risky when you lack domain expertise to validate quality.

Critical Warning #3: Hallucinations Are Real and Convincing

LLMs confidently generate false information that sounds authoritative.

Common hallucination types:

  1. Fake citations: References to papers that don’t exist, with plausible-looking titles and authors
  2. Misattributed arguments: Real papers cited for claims they don’t make
  3. Fabricated facts: Statistics, dates, quotes that sound legitimate but are invented
  4. Blended information: Accurate details mixed with false ones in ways that seem coherent

Real example: AI might cite "Johnson & Smith (2023). ‘Organizational resilience in remote work contexts.’ Journal of Management Studies, 45(3), 287-312."

Sounds legitimate. Could be completely invented. Title, authors, journal, volume, pages—all plausible, all fake.

Why this happens: Models predict plausible text based on patterns. When they don’t know something, they generate what "sounds right" based on similar citations in training data.

Protection strategies:

  1. Always verify citations independently before using them
  2. Cross-reference factual claims with authoritative sources
  3. Be especially skeptical of unexpected or surprising information
  4. Check that sources actually say what AI claims they say
  5. Remember: Confident delivery ≠ accuracy

Fields with automated validation (coding, data analysis with test suites) are more reliable than fields without validation (interpretation, synthesis, argumentation).

For most important work, automated validation doesn’t exist. Treat AI as sophisticated but unreliable assistant, not expert peer.

The Golden Rule: Use AI as Augmentation, Not Replacement

AI works best when augmenting expert judgment, not replacing expertise.

GOOD uses:

  • Researcher: Use AI to find papers you might have missed → you evaluate theoretical contribution
  • Administrator: Use AI to draft policy summary → you verify accuracy against official documents
  • Student: Use AI to explain difficult concept → you test understanding with practice problems
  • Manager: Use AI to format meeting notes → you verify action items are captured correctly

PROBLEMATIC uses:

  • Researcher: Exploring new subfield, have AI generate literature review, can’t assess if key scholars are missing
  • Administrator: Unfamiliar with regulations, have AI draft compliance document, can’t tell if it meets requirements
  • Student: Don’t understand methodology, ask AI to design study, can’t evaluate if approach is appropriate
  • Manager: Use AI to write performance review without deep knowledge of employee’s work

Getting Better Results: Prompt Engineering Basics

Why the way you phrase requests matters:

Remember that LLMs predict the next token based on patterns in their training data. When you’re specific and provide context, you’re helping the model activate the most relevant patterns. Vague prompts lead to generic outputs because the model has to guess which patterns to apply. Think of it like this: "write something" could activate patterns from millions of different contexts, while "write a 200-word technical summary for executives" narrows the space to much more specific patterns the model learned from business communications.

Similarly, providing examples works because you’re showing the model what pattern space you want—you’re essentially saying "activate the statistical patterns that look like THIS, not THAT." Breaking tasks into steps works because the model’s context window is limited—keeping each step focused means the relevant information stays prominent rather than getting diluted across a long, complex request.

Practical techniques:

1. Be specific

  • Bad: "Write about climate change"
  • Better: "Write a 200-word explanation of how ocean currents affect regional climate patterns, suitable for undergraduate students"

Why this works: Narrows which patterns the model draws from (academic explanation) rather than activating everything it knows about climate change.

2. Provide context

  • Include relevant background: "I’m a facilities manager reviewing HVAC proposals…"
  • Specify your constraints: "Budget under $50K, must integrate with existing systems"
  • State your audience: "Explain this for non-technical executives"

Why this works: Helps the model activate patterns appropriate for your role, constraints, and audience rather than generic responses.

3. Use examples

  • Show the format or style you want
  • "Like this: [example], not like this: [example]"
  • Give 2-3 examples if asking for pattern-following

Why this works: You’re directly showing the model which statistical patterns to match—much more effective than trying to describe the pattern in words.

4. Break complex tasks into steps

  • Don’t ask: "Analyze this dataset and create a presentation"
  • Instead: "First, identify the top 5 trends. Then, for each trend…"
  • Let the AI complete one step before moving to the next

Why this works: Keeps the context window focused on one task at a time, preventing relevant details from getting lost in a long, complex prompt. Also gives you an opportunity to tune and provide feedback on a step that might have normally just proceeded without your input—you can catch problems early rather than discovering the entire output was based on a flawed assumption.

5. Iterate and refine

  • First response not quite right? Ask for adjustments
  • "Make it more concise" / "Add more detail about X" / "Rewrite in a more formal tone"
  • Build on what works rather than starting over

Why this works: The previous output is now in the context window—the model can see exactly what to adjust rather than guessing what you want.

6. Ask for the AI’s reasoning

  • "Explain your analysis step-by-step"
  • "What factors did you consider?"

Why this works: Requesting reasoning changes which patterns the model activates—it draws from training data where explanations were provided, not just conclusions.

What doesn’t work:

  • Being intentionally vague to "test" the AI (activates random patterns)
  • Assuming it remembers details from 50 messages ago (context window limits)
  • Expecting it to read your mind about unstated preferences (it only has what you type)

The key insight: Good prompting isn’t about "tricking" the AI—it’s about understanding how pattern-matching systems work and crafting inputs that activate the most useful patterns from training data.

PART 3: MOVING FORWARD (5 minutes)

Practical First Steps

1. Start experimenting thoughtfully

  • Use AI for low-stakes tasks where you can validate output
  • Notice when it helps vs. when it wastes time
  • Develop intuition for where it’s reliable vs. unreliable

2. Verify everything important
